Explanation of Algorithm

Data Gathering

There are 6 datapoints for each person: their gender, and the 5 parts of their personality given by the 5 part personality test found here. These datapoints are used to construct 2 5-dimensional hyperspaces, one for each gender. In addition to the values for each name, there is a list of people that are currently dating.

Meaning of Tables

The table that is visible by default is who the algorithm thinks you should data. The left number is where are they are in your rankings. The greyed out right number is their score. Scores are useless on their own, and should only be used to compare the degree of recommendations: for example, if your number one result has a score of -60, and the number two result -59.5, it doesn't really matter which one of them you date. But if your number one is -30, and the number two is -5, you should definitely date the first person.

If you click the "show confusing table" button, the "confusing" table will show up. It isn't actually confusing, but people don't read instructions, so I hide it by default. That table shows where you are recommended to other people, as the right number. So if the first person in the right table has a right number of 3, you are their number 3 result.

Meaning of colors

The first third of all tables are colored green. These are typically good matches. The second third is black. These are bad matches. The last third is red. These are terrible matches.

People in a relationship are colored yellow as a warning.

Explanation of Ranking Algorithms

Basic Explanation

You know how in math class you can draw a linear regression through points, and use that to predict where a new X would be? I'm basically doing that, but with much more complicated regressions, and 5 dimensions instead of 1.

Caveats

The biggest caveat is that I have only 17 couples in my dataset, representing around 7 "dynamics" - ways a relationship can work, and some of those are filtered by my outlier detection. There are, of course, many more then 7 ways a relationship can work. This means that, while the algorithm can give positives, it is not capable of giving negatives. If someone is high on your list, excluding extenuating circumstances like you're related or they're a freshmen or something, they are probably a good result. But if someone is low on your list, and you think they should be higher because you're dating or something, that doesn't necessarily mean you are not compatible, just that I don't have enough data.

The second caveat is that I don't try to do any kind of age filtering. So seniors sometimes get recommended to freshmen. Don't date freshmen it's creepy.

Accuracy

I test each algorithm for accuracy using something called a leave one out validator. I loop over every couple in my dataset, train a predictive model on every couple except the one I am currently testing, and then see where each partner is recommended to each other. I can then compare algorithms by looking at the average place where partners are recommended to each other.

A purely random algorithm has an accuracy of around 35. Recommending people based off of similarity has an accuracy of around 28. My most accurate regression has an accuracy of around 21. A perfect algorithm would probably have an accuracy of around 5 - not 0, because some people just should not be dating.

So, my algorithm is a little less then twice as accurate as random, and around 4 times worse then a perfect algorithm would be. That's kind of terrible, but I have no way of fixing it without more data. So if you want more accurate results, get me more data.
